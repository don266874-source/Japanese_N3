Bite Size Japanese Podcast (Intermediate) - Ep1: 日本語Podcast

Felix's Learner Note:

While Nihongo con Teppei offers slow-paced monologues packed with everyday Japanese you’ll never find in textbooks, Layla from Bite Size Japanese represents something else entirely. She bridges the gap for learners who haven't fully mastered grammar yet but are ready to transition into listening to natural, continuous speech.

Episode 1 isn’t really a lesson; it’s a re-branding manifesto. It marks her evolution from audio-only to video, and from a "read-along, slow-paced textbook" style to a vlog-style format where she naturally chats about her daily life. The greatest value in shadowing this episode isn’t learning new grammar, but rather training your ears to get used to the natural rhythm of a native speaker talking to you in real-time.

This episode also achieves something few language creators manage to do: she uses her own English-learning journey to prove why podcasts are the ultimate learning medium. She spent 70% of her English study time on podcasts, which is how she completely built up her listening skills. She explains this logic entirely in Japanese, using only N3/N4 level vocabulary.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. 〜ていく / 〜てくる（方向性のある持続）
Meaning: Verb て-form + いく (go on doing / continue into the future) or くる (come doing / have been doing up to now) — marks an action continuing through time, either forward from now or backward from the past.
In Text: podcastを作っていきたいと思います (I'd like to keep making podcasts into the future); 聞いてくれていて (you have been listening up to now, thanks); リスニングがすごく良くなってて (my listening has become really good).
Note: N4. Both 〜ていく and 〜てくる are textbook grammar but their casual-speech use is different — they function as aspect markers rather than physical direction. Self-learners who only learn ていく as "physically going while doing" (歩いていく = go on foot) miss the timeline function entirely. In this episode, Layla uses 〜ていく to express intention ("I want to keep going") and 〜てくる to express experience accumulation ("it has become"). Shadow the 「〜たいと思います」 + 〜ていく + 〜んですけれど chain as a triple-aspect narrative frame. The て-form is doing way more work here than the individual verbs.

2. そのおかげで（感謝の原因・恩恵）
Meaning: "Thanks to that / because of that." その + おかげ + で — marks a positive cause-effect relationship where the result benefits the speaker. Compare: そのせいで = negative cause ("thanks to that [I failed]") vs そのおかげで = positive cause ("thanks to that [I succeeded]").
In Text: そのおかげでリスニングがすごく良くなってて (Thanks to that, my listening got really good); 本当にそのおかげで (Really thanks to that).
Note: N4. Self-learners know おかげ from set phrases ("おかげさまで") but rarely produce the full そのおかげで + positive-result chain when speaking. It is a critical framing device: the speaker is linking a past study habit (podcast 70% of study time) to a present ability (good listening skills). 「おかげで + adjective くなってて」 is a fixed template for "X caused Y to become [adjective]." If you are explaining how you got good at something, this is the phrase you need to open that explanation. Shadow the whole chain as one unit.

3. 全然〜ない（完全否定の強化）
Meaning: "Not at all / completely not." 全然 + negative form — strengthens a full negation. In casual modern speech 全然 can also be used with positive forms ("totally!") but in this episode it's used strictly as a negative amplifier.
In Text: 全然見なくてもいいです (you don't have to watch at all); ぜんぜん見なくてもいいけど (you really don't have to watch at all); used 3 times in the first 2 minutes to give permission and soften obligations.
Note: N4 (but functionally N5). 全然 is one of the first adverbs learners learn, but its usage in this episode is pragmatically nuanced: Layla uses it to give permission, not to deny. 「全然見なくてもいい」 = "you're completely off the hook for watching" — a face-saving move that makes offering optional. Self-learners who only know 全然 from negative-centered sentences ("I don't understand at all") miss its role in making offers non-coercive. Shadow 「全然 + verb なくてもいい」 as a chunk: it is the standard way to say "you don't have to X at all" and sound polite while doing it.

4. 〜に関係した（〜に関係）
Meaning: "Related to / connected with." Noun + に + 関係 + した (past/perfect form of 関係する) + noun — describes relevance between two things. The した form acts as an adjective modifying the following noun.
In Text: 私の日常生活に関係したトピックにしたい (I want to make it about topics related to my daily life); X と Y の 関係 = the relationship between X and Y.
Note: N3. Self-learners often use 〜について ("about") for everything, but 〜に関係した is the precise "relevant to / has to do with" frame that 〜して handles awkwardly. If your Japanese sounds vague when you are describing topics, it is probably because you are using 〜の話 ("a story about") when you mean 〜に関係した話 ("a story related to"). Shadow 「Xに関係したY」 as a fixed frame — it is the core of topic-description in Japanese, and Layla uses it to explain what her podcast will cover.

5. 〜続ける（継続の動詞）
Meaning: "To continue doing / to keep doing." Verb stem (連用形) + 続ける — marks an action that keeps going without stopping. The potential negative 続かない ("won't continue") describes habits that don't stick.
In Text: やっぱり続けることが一番大切ですよね (As expected, continuing is the most important thing!); 私には続かないなと思って (I think it wouldn't continue for me); なかなか新しいことが起きない (new things don't easily happen); 運動不足な状態が続いてしまっている (the lack-of-exercise state continues).
Note: N4. Layla uses 続ける as a self-help concept, not just a grammar point — the key to content creation is not quality, it is persistence. 「やっぱり続けることが一番大切」 is a sentence you will hear constantly from successful creators in Japanese. The negative potential form 「続かない」 is the default way to explain why a habit died: 私には続かない (it doesn't stick for me). Shadow 「V-stem + 続ける」 as a compound-verb pattern, and 続かない as a negative-habit marker. This is how Japanese speakers describe long-term behaviors that require ongoing commitment rather than single-shot effort.
