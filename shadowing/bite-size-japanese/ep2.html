Bite Size Japanese Podcast (Intermediate) - Ep2: I came back to Kyoto

Felix's Learner Note:

The grammar density in this episode is unexpectedly high. Layla hooks the listeners right from the start by sharing a relatable truth—that intermediate and advanced learners are tired of using textbooks—instantly drawing everyone into her perspective. She then spends the next five minutes unfolding a narrative about a "failed trip." The five core structures she uses form the absolute backbone of spoken travel Japanese:〜てしまう / 〜ちゃう (Te-shimau / Chau): Expresses regret or accidental actions (e.g., 調べないで行っちゃった — going there without doing any research beforehand).〜し (Shi): Used for listing multiple reasons (e.g., わからないし、使いたくないし、したくないし — because I don't know, and I don't want to use it, and I don't want to do it).〜ていない / 〜てない (Te-inai / Tenai): Indicates an ongoing state of incompletion (e.g., 全然わかってない、まだ慣れてない — not understanding at all, not used to it yet).〜とか (Toka): Used for casual enumeration or giving examples (e.g., 秋は紅葉とか、オレンジとか赤とか — in autumn, things like autumn leaves, or orange, or red).〜じゃないかな (Janai ka na): A soft, polite way to make a suggestion (e.g., 田舎おすすめじゃないかな — I think the countryside might be a good recommendation)

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. 〜てしまう / 〜ちゃう（不可逆・残念な結果）
Meaning: "End up doing ~ / did ~ (with regret or accident)." Verb て-form + しまう — marks completion, often with a tone of "I did it, and now it's done, and I'd rather not have." In casual speech てしまう → ちゃう.
In Text: 調べないで行っちゃったから (I went without looking anything up, and that was a mistake); 何も見れなかった (I couldn't see anything); 交通費に4000円から5000円使いました (I ended up spending 4000-5000 yen on transportation); 高かった (it was expensive).
Note: N4. 〜てしまう is the emotional core of this episode. Layla's entire Mt. Hiei story is a てしまう chain: didn't research → went → couldn't see anything → spent a lot → it was expensive. Each てしまう is a small regret that compounds into a big travel failure. Self-learners learn てしまう from textbooks as "doing something completely" (e.g., 食べてしまいました = "ate it all up") but the dominant use in this episode is the "unwanted result" frame. Shadow the 調べないで行っちゃったから chain as a unit; it's how Japanese speakers narrate a cascade of small mistakes.

2. 〜し（理由・情報の並列）
Meaning: "And / besides / what's more." Verb/い-adjective short form + し, repeated — lists multiple reasons or pieces of information without specifying which is the main one. The implication is "and there are other reasons too."
In Text: 教科書を使いたくないし (I don't want to use textbooks, and); そんなに気にしたくないし (I don't want to worry about that so much, and); やっぱりたくさん話す練習が楽しい (as expected, lots of speaking practice is fun); 全然わかってないし (I don't understand at all, and); 古い家も多いし (there are many old houses, and).
Note: N4. 〜し is the most natural way to list reasons in spoken Japanese. Unlike 〜から (because) or 〜ので (since), 〜し doesn't force you to pick a single cause — it lets you pile up multiple reasons, each one adding weight. Self-learners who only know 〜から often sound like they're making a single-point argument when they want to express a general feeling. Shadow the 教科書を使いたくないし、〜気にしたくないし、〜話す練習が楽しい chain; it's how Japanese speakers build a case without being argumentative.

3. 〜ていない / 〜てない（未完了の状態）
Meaning: "Have not yet done / still not doing." Verb ていない form — marks an action that has not been completed or a state that has not been achieved. The ない ending makes it negative, the ている form makes it a state.
In Text: 全然わかってないし (I don't understand at all); まだ慣れてないところがあったりして (there are still places I'm not used to yet); 何も見れなかった (I couldn't see anything); 何もリサーチ、検索とか調べないで行っちゃった (I went without doing any research or searching).
Note: N4. 〜ていない is the standard "haven't done yet" form, but in casual speech it often contracts to 〜てない. The difference between 〜ない (don't do) and 〜ていない (haven't done yet) is crucial: 〜ない is a general statement ("I don't eat meat"), 〜ていない is a state that implies you might do it later ("I haven't eaten yet"). Layla uses 〜てない to describe her English ability: 全然わかってない = "I still don't understand" (implying she's still learning). Shadow the 全然わかってないし chain; it's how Japanese speakers describe ongoing gaps in ability.

4. 〜とか（緩やかな列挙）
Meaning: "Things like ~ / ~ and such." Noun or verb short form + とか + noun/verb + とか — lists items casually as representative examples, not an exhaustive list. Softer than や and more casual than など.
In Text: 秋は紅葉とか (autumn is autumn leaves and such); 黄色とかオレンジとか赤とか茶色になって (turn yellow, orange, red, brown); 京都とか東京とかだけじゃなくて (not just Kyoto or Tokyo); 観光客とか誰もいないけど (there are no tourists or anyone); 200年前に、とか100年前に住んでいた (living 200 years ago, or 100 years ago).
Note: N4. とか is the single most common listing device in spoken Japanese, and this episode uses it in two distinct patterns: noun listing (紅葉とか、黄色とか) and temporal listing (200年前とか、100年前とか). The difference from たり is subtle: たり implies "among other things I did A and B," while とか implies "A, B, that's the sort of thing." Self-learners who know たり from textbooks often overuse it and neglect とか. Shadow the 秋は紅葉とか、黄色とかオレンジとか chain; it's how Japanese speakers describe colors, seasons, and categories without sounding like a textbook.

5. 〜じゃないかな（柔らかい提案・推測）
Meaning: "I wonder if ~ / probably ~ / might be ~." A soft-assertion hedge that states an opinion while leaving room for the listener's view. The かな adds a wondering tone, the じゃない makes it a negative question (softer than a direct assertion).
In Text: 私はオススメしないかな (I might not recommend it); 比叡山は本当におすすめかな (Mt. Hiei is really recommended, I wonder); 田舎おすすめですよ (the countryside is recommended); そんな気がします (I have that feeling).
Note: N4. じゃないかな is the most important soft-assertion pattern that self-learners under-use because they are trained to speak in positive/declarative forms. Compare: きっと〜です ("certainly is") → 〜じゃないですか ("isn't it?") → 〜じゃないかな ("I wonder?"). The じゃないかな form is safest for cultural contexts because it makes no claims about facts, only about guesses. Layla uses it to recommend and de-recommend: オススメしないかな = "I might not recommend it" (but you can decide for yourself). Shadow the オススメしないかな chain; it's how Japanese speakers make recommendations without sounding pushy.
