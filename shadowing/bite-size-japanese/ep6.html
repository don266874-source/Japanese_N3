Bite Size Japanese Podcast (Intermediate) - Ep6: 2021年に失敗したこと

Felix's Learner Note:

Six episodes in and Layla's podcast has found its stride: equal parts personal story, practical grammar, and cultural commentary. This one is her year-end review — literally. She scans 2021 and emerges with a brutally honest diagnosis: she succeeded at Instagram (160,000 followers from zero) but failed at everything that Instagram demanded in return. She obsessed over the numbers, checking the app compulsively and, when her count dropped, feeling too afraid to open it at all. She lost her own time. She couldn't draw, couldn't read, couldn't exercise. And then, in the final minutes, she drops the news that she's bought a 200-year-old house in the Japanese coastal town of Tomonoura (the setting of Ghibli's Ponyo) and intends to spend the next three years renovating it into a business space. It is the most unexpectedly hopeful episode of the series.

What makes this episode a grammar workout is the density of N4 structures self-learners almost never see modeled in personal-confession register. Five stand out. First, 〜すぎる in its full emotional range: 頑張りすぎた (tried too hard), 考えすぎ (overthink), 見すぎてしまっている (end up watching too much). Second, 〜による for both cause and variation: ソーシャルメディアのせいです (it's social media's fault) vs. 国や家庭によって全然違う (totally different depending on country and family). Third, 〜ことがある to mark intermittent experiences: 開けられないこともありました (there were times I couldn't open it). Fourth, 〜たい to lay out a future self-portrait: 絵をかきたい、運動もしたい、本も読みたい (I want to draw, exercise, read more). Fifth, 〜ように努める for ongoing effort: 気をつけたいと思います (I want to be more careful). Every sentence is a chunk you can steal for your own confessions.

Grammatically, the shadowing value triples when you notice how Layla handles conditionals. Her central 「3年後に死ぬって言われたら」 thought experiment — "if I were told I'd die in 3 years" — usesたら form for an unreal conditional, chains it with 絶対やめる (I'd absolutely quit), and contrasts that with 今は若いし〜したい (I'm still young and want to pursue). It is the textbook pattern for "what I would do if X, but what I'm actually doing is Y" delivered in real, messy, spoken Japanese. If you have ever described your own ambitions vs. your current reality in Japanese, this is the episode to internalize.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. 〜すぎる（やりすぎ・考えすぎ・見すぎ）
Meaning: "Too much / to excess." Verb stem or adjective stem + すぎる — marks that a state or action has crossed a reasonable boundary.
In Text: ちょっと頑張りすぎました (I tried a bit too hard); 数を気にしすぎた (cared too much about numbers); 見すぎてしまっている (end up watching too much); 考えすぎ (overthinking).
Note: N4. すぎる is the most load-bearing "overdo" marker in spoken Japanese. Self-learners place it after the full verb form ("飲むすぎる" instead of "飲みすぎる"), which is always wrong — remove the dictionary-form る, attach すぎる: 飲む→飲みすぎる, 頑張る→頑張りすぎる. The てしまう compound (見すぎてしまっている) adds a tone of "and I wish I hadn't." Shadow all three forms (頑張りすぎた、気にしすぎた、見すぎてしまっている) as a unit; the shift from physical action to mental habit to screen addiction is how Japanese speakers apologize for their own excesses.

2. 〜による（原因・手段・変動条件）
Meaning: "Due to / by means of / depending on." Noun + による + result — marks cause, means, or the condition that changes the outcome.
In Text: ソーシャルメディアのせいです (it's because of social media) vs. 国や家庭によって全然違う (varies completely by country and family); 修理をする時に、3年後に始めるサービスの内容に合わせて、インテリアをデザインする必要があります — the design must match the service content, determined by に合わせて, but によって sets up the cultural comparison.
Note: N4. による is a three-purpose grammar point that self-learners almost never master in one piece. (1) Cause: 台風による欠格 (cancellation due to typhoon). (2) Means: バスによる移動 (travel by bus). (3) Variation: 文化によって違う (differs by culture). Layla uses sense (3) for Christmas (〜によって全然違う) and sense (1) implicitly for social media (〜のせいです = it's X's fault). The key is that による either explains why something happened or explains why something differs. Shadow 「Noun + によって + different/caused result」 as a cultural-comparison template.

3. 〜ことがある（経験・機会・ occasion）
Meaning: "There are times when / sometimes / have done before." Verb short form + ことがある — marks non-regular occurrence or past experience.
In Text: 開けられないこともありました (there were times I couldn't open it); きついこともありました (there were also times it was hard); 体調が悪いこともありました (there were also times my physical condition was bad).
Note: N4. Self-learners often use ときどき ("sometimes") when ことがある is the natural choice for non-habitual experience. ときどき = regularly sometimes; ことがある = occasionally / there have been occasions. The past form こともあった (there were times when) is the standard for difficult experiences you've had but don't have anymore. Shadow the テ-form (〜ていた時もあったし、〜ないこともありました) — this exact chain is how Japanese speakers narrate ups and downs in a story without overclaiming frequency.

4. 〜たい（希望・願望）
Meaning: "Want to do / would like to." Verb stem (remove る/ます) + たい — expresses speaker's desire. To ask about another person's desire, add ですか or か.
In Text: 自分の時間をもっと作りたい (I want to make more of my own time); 絵をかきたいし、運動もしたい、本も読みたい (I want to draw, exercise, read more); 仕事がしたい (I want to do work); 探していますね (I'm searching for it — want innuendo).
Note: N4 (but functionally N5). たい is the most basic desire-marker in Japanese, yet the common error is using it for other people's desires ("彼は食べたい" can only mean "I think he wants to eat" if you're inside his head — otherwise use たがっている). Layla stacks たい three times in a row (絵をかきたい、運動もしたい、本も読みたい) to build a portrait of desired future self. The stacking is the grammar move to imitate. Also note the 〜たいと思います softener: 頑張りたいですね, 気をつけたいと思います — it makes desire sound less demanding.

5. 〜っぽい（傾向・特有の性質）
Meaning: "-like / tending to." Verb stem, adjective stem, or noun + っぽい — describes a quality, tendency, or resemblance. Can be positive or negative depending on context.
In Text: オレンジっぽい (orange-ish); 子供っぽい (childish); 忘れっぽい (forgetful); 安っぽい (cheap-looking). This episode uses っぽい in passing during descriptions of taste, color, and self-characterization.
Note: N4. っぽい is the most playful spoken-Japanese suffix and self-learners underuse it because it never appears in formal textbook lists. It attaches: verbs → 怒りっぽい (quick to anger), i-adjectives → 甘っぽい (a bit sweet), na-adj/nouns → 嘘っぽい (sounds like a lie). Layla's usage is casual and tonal — when she describes herself or her own habits (考えすぎっぽい、単調っぽい), she's doing self-aware humor. Shadow 安っぽい / 安い as the minimal-pair demonstration of "cheap" vs "cheap-looking"; in Japanese, the distinction is exact.
