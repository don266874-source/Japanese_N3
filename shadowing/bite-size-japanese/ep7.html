Bite Size Japanese Podcast (Intermediate) - Ep7: 日本にはオーブンがない？

Felix's Learner Note:

This is the shortest and most culturally-packed episode of the Bite size Japanese podcast, and it may be the single most useful 5 minutes for anyone planning to live in Japan. Layla dismantles the vocabulary gap between English and Japanese kitchen terms: oven (オーブン) doesn't really exist in Japan, only オーブンレンジ — a microwave-sized box that bakes poorly. オーブントースター is a regular toaster. "Stove" in English = コンロ in Japanese, but ストーブ in Japanese means heater. The kitchen is narrow, has only two burners, a tiny dishwasher that isn't built-in, and no room to cook for more than two people. If you are a self-learner planning to rent an apartment in Japan, this episode is survival Japanese: the grammar is almost entirely N5/N4, the vocabulary is kitchen-critical, and the cultural context (why Japanese kitchens are designed this way) gives you a framework for your own future complaints.

Four grammar structures carry the episode. First, 〜じゃない / 〜じゃありません in its full denial register: オーブンが全然ない (there are absolutely no ovens); オーブンじゃない (it's not a real oven). Second, 〜って (と) as a casual quotation marker: 「ストーブ」って言ったら (if you say "stove"); オーブンレンジという名前で (by the name "oven range"). Third, 〜時だけ / 〜ときの (time/duration): クリスマス予定がある時 (when you have Christmas plans); 曇りの日は (on cloudy days). Fourth, できるか〜ないか for ability impossibility: できるけど (you can do it, but); 絶対無理 (absolutely impossible). Every sentence is a self-contained cultural observation that you can also use when your own kitchen frustrations demand words.

Two things that make this episode a pedagogical gem: Layla's self-inventory of her own mood tied to weather (いい日は元気、曇りの日はテンションが下がる) and her catastrophic honesty about cooking a Yorkshire pudding without an oven. Both are models for self-learners who need to narrate how environment affects their emotions and how to admit failure without drama. Also note いかにも Japanese phrases like 「お祝いしろ！」って — that exact confrontational quote is something non-Japanese speakers rarely hear modeled, and it's the grammar of Japanese social pressure.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. 〜って（＝と：引用・定義）
Meaning: "Called / named / that." The casual spoken form of quotation と, used to introduce terms, names, or quotes.
In Text: 「ストーブ」って言ったら (if you say 'stove'); オーブンレンジという名前で (by the name oven range); 「お祝いしろ！」って (the quote 'celebrate!').
Note: N4. Self-learners know と as the written quotation marker, but って is the spoken default and carries roughly 80% of everyday quotation functions. The difference is register: と is "he said that X" (neutral report); って is "he said X" (conversational, sometimes emphatic). Shadow 「Word」って + verb; this is the most common way Japanese speakers define terms mid-sentence.
2. 〜じゃない / 〜じゃありません（否定の断定）
Meaning: "Is not / are not." The casual (じゃない) and polite (じゃありません) negative copula for nouns and na-adjectives.
In Text: オーブンじゃない (it's not an oven); 好きじゃなかった (didn: 't like it); オーブンが全然ない (there are absolutely no ovens); それが英語のtensionの意味じゃない (that's not the English meaning of tension).
Note: N5. じゃない is the most basic negative in Japanese, but this episode shows its emotional range: denial (オーブンじゃない), past dislike (好きじゃなかった), absolute absence (全然ない), and meaning correction (意味じゃない). The pattern 全然ない = "absolutely does not exist" is key for complaints. Shadow these four uses as a set — they are the daily-negative backbone.
3. 〜時（とき）/ 〜時は（時間帯・条件）
Meaning: "When / at the time of." Noun/Sentence + 時 (とき) marks a time or condition when something happens or is true.
In Text: もうちょっとでクリスマス (almost Christmas = the time is coming); クリスマス予定がある時 (when you have Christmas plans); 曇りの日は (on cloudy days — time + は contrast marker); 今の生活 (current life/time); 3年後に死ぬって言われたら (if I was told I'd die in 3 years — hypothetical time).
Note: N4. 時 is the "when" connector. The form is: Verb short form + 時, Noun + の + 時. Self-learners often overuse たら for time and underuse 時. 時 marks "during/at the time of"; たら marks "if/when (conditional trigger)." Shadow Noun + の + 時 + は for "at this time, (specific situation)" — it's the standard context-setting frame.
4. 〜できる / 〜できない（可能・不可能）
Meaning: "Can / cannot / possible / impossible." Verb dictionary form + できる (potential form) marks that an action is within the agent's ability.
In Text: オーブンとして使うこともできます (you can also use it as an oven); 焼くことはできるけど (you can bake it, but); 絶対無理 (absolutely impossible); できるか〜ないか (can or cannot — for the pizza question).
Note: N4. できる is the most common way to express "can" by attaching to any verb (verb stem + できる). The negative is できない (cannot). In casual speech, 無理 (muri) = "impossible" is the default for physical impossibility: 絶対無理 = "absolutely impossible." Shadow the できるけど + 無理 chain; this is the standard "yes you can, but in this specific case it's impossible" conversation pattern.
5. 〜という〜（名前・レッテル）
Meaning: "Called / named / labeled as." The pattern という links a name, label, or description to what it describes.
In Text: オーブンレンジという名前で (by the name 'oven range'); オーブントースターということもあります (it's also called 'oven toaster'); 社会に言われている感じがする (feels like I'm being told by society); ハイテンションっていう言葉がある (there's a word called 'high tension').
Note: N4. という is the "quote frame" and "name frame" in one: A という B = "B called A" or "B that is A." Self-learners who know という only as a quotation tool miss its role as a name-labeler. The pattern っていう (casual) is the spoken default. Shadow っていう言葉がある / っていう感じがする; these are two of the most frequent spoken-Japanese sentence patterns.
