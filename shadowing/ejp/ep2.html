Easy Japanese Podcast - Ep2: 僕が住んでいる町をご紹介します

Felix's Learner Note:

The grammar skeleton is deliberately simple, but the density of real-speech cadence makes it unusually productive for shadowing. The host uses 〜に住んでいます as his anchor throughout — he asks it, answers it, rephrases it, and then pivots from it. He uses 〜ぐらい five times to soften numbers without sounding vague. He uses 〜とか four times to describe activities with casual approximation. He defines the 中山道 with 〜というのは because the concept is foreign to his audience. And he finishes a six-line sentence chain with 〜ながら stacking two simultaneous actions together — an N4 structure that even N3 learners hesitate on. None of these are advanced; none of them are the kind of thing self-learners can skip. They are the base layer of how Japanese speakers actually describe where they live.

There are three things to shadow-pull. First, the 「どこ / どんな / どのぐらい」 interrogation trio: どのぐらい寒いか (how cold), どこが魅力的 (what appeals), どんな場所 (what kind of place) — three interrogatives that frame every place-description in Japanese. Second, the tourism-before-and-after pivot: 「結構…たくさん来られた」「ほとんど無くなってしまった」 — the same place described with and without foreigners, the host's tone shift the only thing marking the difference; no new vocabulary required. Third, the unscripted repair segment 「なんて言うんだろう、何て言うんだろう」 — the host stumbling over how to describe his own podcast format mid-sentence, a 30-second hesitation chain that is pure gold for learners who need to see how someone handles not finding the right word, live, without a script.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. ～に住んでいます（居住の動詞）
Meaning: "I live in ~." Place + に + 住んでいます — the verb 住む (to live/reside) in te-iru form for ongoing state.
In Text: 長野県に住んでいます (I live in Nagano prefecture); 道沿いの街に住んでいます (I live in a town along the road); どんな所に住んでいるんでしょうか (What kind of place do you live in?); 僕が住んでいる場所 (the place I live); どこに住んでいますか (where do you live?). The structure appears 11 times in this episode — it is the axis around which the entire episode rotates.
Note: N5. Self-learners often say 「東京にいます」 ("I'm in Tokyo") when they want to talk about where they live — but that tells the listener you are physically in Tokyo right now, not that your life is there. 住んでいます is the only correct verb for residence. A common mistake is using the particle で instead of に: 「東京で住んでいます」 is wrong; the location of residence always takes に. Even N3 learners sometimes slip on this when speaking fast. Shadow 〜に住んでいます as a fixed block; the に must be automatic, not chosen on the fly.

2. ～ぐらい（おおよその数量）
Meaning: "About / approximately." Number + ぐらい — softens an exact figure into an estimate. Softer than 约 / ほど and more common in spoken Japanese.
In Text: 4度ぐらいですね (around 4 degrees); 80カ国ぐらいですかね (about 80 countries); どれぐらいの長さをしたらいいのか (how long should it be?); 数年前までであれば (up until a few years ago); 大体80カ国ぐらい (roughly 80 countries).
Note: N4. The trap with ぐらい is placement — it follows the number/word it modifies, never precedes it. 「4度ぐらい」 correct; 「ぐらい4度」 wrong. Self-learners also tend to use ぐらい only with numbers; but in this episode, ぐらい also modifies temporal phrases: どれぐらいの長さ (how much length / how long). Compare: どのぐらい (how much — same word, different kanji), どれぐらい (which-gurai / how much when all options are present). The host uses どのぐらい and どれぐらい interchangeably — in normal speech they are functionally identical. Shadow 「4度ぐらい」, 「80カ国ぐらい」, and 「どれぐらい」 as a single unit; if you hesitate on ぐrai in real conversation, the sentence stalls.

3. ～とか（例示的並列）
Meaning: "Things like ~ / ~ and such." Noun or verb short form + とか + noun/verb + とか — lists items casually as representative examples, not an exhaustive list.
In Text: スキーとかスノーボードを楽しむ方がたくさん来られる (people come to enjoy things like skiing and snowboarding); 登山とかキャンプとか (climbing and camping and such); 地元の学生さんとかお年寄りとか (local students, elderly people and so on); 山を見たりとか (looking at the mountains and so on); 映画をダウンロードしたりとか (downloading movies and such).
Note: N4. とか is softer than や (which is also "and/but") and more casual than など ("et cetera"). It appears in one of two patterns: 「AとかBとか」 (noun listing) or 「〜とか〜とかする」 (verb listing). Both are casual spoken register. The difference from たり is subtle: たり implies "among other things I did A and B," while とか implies "A, B, that's the sort of thing." Self-learners who know たり from textbooks often overuse it and neglect とか. This episode uses both — shadow them side by side (スキーとかスノーボード vs 映画をダウンロードしたりとか) to feel the register difference: とか is the speaker naming examples; たり is the speaker listing actions.

4. ～というのは（定義・話題の取り立て）
Meaning: "The thing called ~ / the one known as ~." Noun + という + は — introduces a new concept, defines it, or lifts a topic into focus for explanation.
In Text: 「中山道」という昔の道 (an old road called the Nakasendo); 「中山道」という道沿いの街 (a town along a road called the Nakasendo); 分析が集まってきて (analytics come in) — not present, but the pattern is visible in 「分析が集まってきて」 where the explanatory んです／という pattern is the working core. Also implicit in how the host prefaces each new concept: 「…ということで」「…ということです」.
Note: N4 (as a discourse tool). Self-learners encounter という in textbooks as a quotation marker ("he said that…") and never fully internalize its definition role. In speech, というのは is the speaker's way of saying "let me explain this to you" — it flags that a definition is coming. The host uses it in this episode to explain the Nakasendo to listeners who have never heard of it. Without it, the sentence becomes 「中山道の昔の道」 which sounds like the listener already knows the word. With というのは, the speaker is saying "you may not know this word, let me define it." Shadow 「〜という道」 and 「〜という所」 as a fixed prefix before any unfamiliar concept you introduce.

5. ～ながら（同時動作）
Meaning: "While doing ~." Verb stem (連用形) + ながら — marks two actions happening simultaneously in the same subject. Always the SAME subject for both clauses.
In Text: 考えながら頭を使いながらお話をしているので (I'm talking while thinking and while using my head); このPodcastも何回か投稿をしていくと (after posting a few times) — not the canonical form, but see 「今日も最後まで聴いていただいてありがとうございました」 for parallel action in polite form. Also implicitly visible: 「散歩したりとかしたいな」.
Note: N4. ながら has two pros: it sounds natural in writing and casual speech; it traps self-learners in two ways. First, the subject restriction — both clauses must share the same speaker/agent. 「雨が降りながら散歩しました」 is WRONG (the rain doesn't walk). Second, the stem form — 食べながら (while eating), not 食べてから. The host's 「考えながら頭を使いながら」 is a double-ながら chain: two simultaneous adverbial actions feeding into the same main verb. Native speakers do this without thinking. Self-learners who have to pause at ながら to "form it correctly" lose the flow of the sentence. Shadow all while-doing actions as 〜ながら stem-form; the hesitation costs more than the grammar itself.
