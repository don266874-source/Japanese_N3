Easy Japanese Podcast - Ep3: 今日はとても寒いです

Felix's Learner Note:

What makes this episode unusually good for targeted shadowing is the density of two emotional registers that self-learners almost never practice together. The first is 〜すぎると — "when X is too much, Y happens" — a six-line chain where the host describes a causal cascade from cold weather through loss of motivation, freezing ground, inability to run, frustration, and the final consolation of indoor exercises. The second is 久しぶり + 〜たことがある — structures marking experience and elapsed time — the host's 「実は結構久しぶりのことですね」「何年振りかな」 says more about COVID-era melancholy than any of the weather words. Together, these two register clusters cover more real conversational Japanese than most full textbooks dedicate to "weather and daily routine" chapters combined.

There are three rhythms to isolate. First, the 5:30am timeline narration: 朝起きて窓のところですね、窓の内側が氷張ってましたね、かなり寒かったんだなあという風に思う — a pure shadow-practice rhythm of observation → description → realization that repeats the basic narrative unit in spoken Japanese. Second, the routine-as-inventory chain: 掃除をしたりとか〜ゴミを捨てたりとかね、そういうことをするんですけれども — the default-shape self-learners use to describe "I do things like..." But notice what comes next: 寒すぎるとね、どうしてもそういうやる気もなくなっちゃいますよね — the concession to self-honesty that makes this register real. Third, the Japanese-style room self-description 「畳の上にね布団を敷いて寝ている」 — a noun-modifier chain that needs rhythm to sound native. Shadow all three and you will be closer to a spoken-weather-for-daily-life register than any N5 grammar list gets you on its own.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. 〜すぎると / 〜すぎて（過度・原因）
Meaning: "When something is excessive, it..." — い-adjective (stem: 〜すぎる) / な-adjective + すぎる used to express "too much" and often followed by an undesirable consequence.
In Text: 寒すぎるとね、どうしてもそういうやる気もなくなっちゃいますよね (When it's too cold, you just lose motivation for that stuff); 寒すぎるとね (when it's too cold); 寒くて目が覚めました (I woke up from the cold).
Note: N4. すぎる attaches to verb stem (remove る/ます from verbs) or adjective stem: 寒い → 寒すぎる, 忙しい → 忙しすぎる, 食べる → 食べすぎる. When the speaker says すぎると, what follows is almost always something negative — the "excessive state triggers an unwanted result" pattern. Self-learners often use すぎる in isolated examples ("too big," "too small") but struggle with the full すぎると + consequence chain. In this episode, 寒すぎるとね sets up the entire second half — cold → loss of motivation → inability to run → frustration. Shadow the 寒すぎるとね + やる気もなくなっちゃいます chain as a unit; this is how Japanese speakers link weather complaints to personal consequences.

2. 〜たことがある / 〜たことがない（経験と未経験）
Meaning: "I have (never) done ~." Past tense short form + ことがある marks that the experience exists / doesn't exist for the speaker. More flexible than 〜たきり (which means "since then, hasn't").
In Text: 海外旅行に行くことが多いです (I often go abroad); 走ることはできない (I can't run); 実は結構久しぶりのことですね (Actually it's the first time in a long while); 何年振りかな (I wonder how many years it's been); 最近のお勧めの映画、ドラマありますかね (Do you have any recent recommended movies or dramas?).
Note: N4. The host uses both たことがある ≈ "have done" and たことがない ≈ "have never done" to frame his experience narrative. Key distinction: 〜たことがない is "I have never done" whereas 〜たことがある is "I have done (at least once)." The 「久しぶり」 frame supplies elapsed-time context: "It's been a long time since I [experienced this]." Self-learners confuse this with 〜ている (currently doing) or 〜ました (did once at a specific time). The ことがある form says nothing about when — only that the experience (or lack of it) exists. Shadow 「海外旅行に行くことが多いです」「出会った人のレシピを覚えたりとかして」「日本に帰ってきてから実際に作ってみて」「友だちに食べてもらったりする」 as a rhythm of experience-grammar in spoken recounting.

3. 〜ちゃう / 〜てしまう（不可逆の残念）
Meaning: Something done completely, often with a sense of regret, accident, or irreversibility. In casual speech てしまう → ちゃう (ては → ちゃ).
In Text: やる気もなくなっちゃいます (My motivation just disappears); 観光も人が来ないので / 冬は結構暇だよっていう方が多い (People say they have a lot of free time in winter); 雪が降ってしまうと (Once it snows); 地面が氷になってしまって (The ground turns to ice); 運動不足な状態が続いてしまって (Being in a lack-of-exercise state continues).
Note: N4. 〜てしまう appears repeatedly in this episteme as an "unwanted result" frame — snow → ice → can't run → frustration → no choice. The host uses both て-form (snowが降ってしまうと / 地面が氷になってしまって) and ちゃ-form (やるかもなくなっちゃいます). Self-learners learn てしまう from textbooks as "doing something completely" (e.g., 食べてしまいました = "ate it all up") but the dominant use in this episode is negative-trigger — the ground freezes (not because anyone wanted it to). Shadow 「雪が降ってしまうと」「地面が氷になってしまって」「ツルツル滑るので」「なかなか走ることはできない」 as a four-line consequence chain; the しまう form is the emotional anchor of the "it happened and I couldn't stop it" register.

4. 〜ちゃう系：〜なくなる / 〜なくなる（状態変化）
Meaning: "Becomes no longer ~" — the negative potential pivot. You start with a state or ability, and なくなる signals its disappearance / loss of viability.
In Text: やる気もなくなっちゃいます (Even motivation disappears); 見かけることはほぼ無くなってしまったんですが (Seeing them has almost completely disappeared); 出来ないくて〜イライラする (Can't do it, and it's frustrating); うん / そう / うん (discourse markers pacing the complaint).
Note: N4. なくなる is the most important negative-state-change verb in spoken Japanese. When paired with てしまう (なくなる → なくなってしまう → なくなっちゃう), it becomes the standard "unwanted loss" construction. Self-learners sometimes just say ありません (doesn't exist) when they should be using なくなった (disappeared / became no longer), creating a static sentence where the listener expected a change-of-state narrative. In this episode, the loss-motivation chain (寒すぎるとね → やる気もなくなっちゃいますよね) is the exact template learners need: condition → emotional loss → external action stopped. Also note the host uses this when talking about tourists disappearing: ほぼ無くなってしまった — the "used to be, now isn't" pivot in a single verb.

5. 〜じゃないかな（不確かな推測・柔らかい断定）
Meaning: "I wonder if ~ / It's probably ~, right?" — a soft-assertion hedge used to state an opinion while leaving room for listeners to add their own read. Shows up in the negative-question register.
In Text: 1度ぐらいじゃないかな (I wonder if it's about 1 degree?); 寒いところも結構あるんじゃないかなと思うんですけれども (I think there are probably colder places); 冬は結構暇だよっていう方が多いんじゃないかな (I think many people say they have a lot of free time in winter); 新しいことをやる時間に当てるっていうのが一番いいんじゃないかな (I think maybe putting time into new things is best); 皆さんの方が詳しいんじゃないかな (I think you all might know more).
Note: N4. じゃないかな is the most important soft-assertion pattern that self-learners under-use because they are trained to speak in positive/declarative forms. Compare: きっと〜です ("certainly is") to 〜じゃないですか ("isn't it?") to 〜じゃないかな ("I wonder?"). The じゃないかな form is safest for cultural contexts because it makes no claims about facts, only about guesses. The host uses it 6+ times in this episode alone — every time he's making a claim about temperature, about visitors, about listener knowledge. Self-learners who don't internalize じゃないかな end up sounding overconfident in one direction or too vague in the other.
