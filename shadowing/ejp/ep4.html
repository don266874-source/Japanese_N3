Easy Japanese Podcast - Ep4: 日本語の勉強のやり方

Felix's Learner Note:

Episode four of EASY JAPANESE is the one learners come back to after a week of exhausting grammar study and say "this should have been the first thing I heard." The host, who has studied English and Spanish as foreign languages himself, shares the core technique he uses to make any new language actually usable: fill your head with the target language, point at things and name them immediately in the target language, and narrate your actions as you do them — without translating through Japanese. 「今、僕はパソコンの前に座っています」「今、僕は椅子に座っています」「今、僕は玄関のドアを開けます」 — a minute-long monologue of pure self-talk in Japanese, built around the exact things he is doing as he speaks. The technique is easy to describe and brutal to actually do until it clicks. And the language he uses to explain this technique is the very first N4+ language you can actually process as a learner: simple verbs, repeated sentence frames, and vocabulary that physical objects will teach you in a single week of point-and-speak practice.

What makes this episode an essential shadowing resource is the side-by-side demonstration of the "wrong" way and the "right" way to process new words. The host explicitly compares 「これは紙です」 said as a direct realisation versus 「これは紙です」 said as a translation of "this is paper" through English/your native language. 「トランスレーションしてしまうと、どうしても一回自分の言葉で考えて、それを日本語に変換しているような形になる」 — trying to translate creates a factory production line in your head where the product comes out slower than the demand. The direct way — いきなり「これは紙です」で考える — requires a mental leap that most self-learners resist because it means front-loading vocabulary memorization before the language feels "comfortable." The episode makes the pitch for doing exactly that.

There are three structural rhythms to shadow. First, the 〜ながら / 〜ながらやっていく chain — "doing while doing" — how to narrate body actions while performing them in real time: 歩きながらしゃべる, 聞きながらノートを取る, やりながらやっていく. Second, the いきなり X です rhythm — the instant naming reflex: 紙です、りんごです、牛乳です. The host piles these on top of each other without any modifier or context, exactly as you will if you practice direct-naming every object you see. Third, the その場合は〜ですね pivot — when you cannot do the high-level version (because you do not yet know the verbs), fall back to the low-level version (naming nouns) until the high-level version becomes possible. That fallback plan is the episode's most practical gift to beginners.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. 〜ながら（同時動作）
Meaning: "While doing ~." Verb stem (連用形) + ながら — marks two actions happening simultaneously in the same subject.
In Text: 歩いてる時とかに私は今歩いています (while walking, I'm walking now); 歩きながらやっていく (doing while walking); 考えながら頭を使いながらお話をしている (talking while thinking and using my head); やりながらやっていくと (if you do it while doing it); 聞きながらノートを取る (take notes while listening).
Note: N4. ながら is the single most useful "simultaneous action" marker in spoken Japanese, and this episode is a masterclass in its use. The host uses it to describe his own study method: narrating actions while performing them. The key restriction: both clauses must share the same subject. 「雨が降りながら散歩しました」 is wrong. The stem form is required: 歩きながら (not 歩ってから). Self-learners often hesitate at ながら because they try to "form it correctly" mid-sentence. Shadow the 考えながら頭を使いながらお話をしている chain as a unit; it's how Japanese speakers describe their own thought process in real time.

2. いきなり（突然・すぐに）
Meaning: "Suddenly / right away / without hesitation." An adverb that marks an immediate action without preparation or translation.
In Text: いきなり「これは紙です」で考える (think "this is paper" right away); いきなり日本語で考えるのがポイント (the key point is to think in Japanese right away); これは神ですっていきなり考えることが大事 (it's important to think "this is paper" suddenly); いきなり日本語で考える (think in Japanese right away).
Note: N4. いきなり is the host's favorite word in this episode — he uses it 4 times to emphasize the "direct naming" method. The contrast is with トランスレーション (translation): the wrong way is to think in your native language first, then convert; the right way is いきなり — immediate, no middle step. Self-learners often resist this because it feels "too hard" without the translation crutch. But いきなり is the difference between a fluent speaker and a translator. Shadow the いきなり X です rhythm as a fixed chunk; it's the core practice technique of the entire episode.

3. 〜てしまう（不可逆・残念）
Meaning: "End up doing ~ / do ~ (with a sting of regret or accident)." Verb て-form + しまう — marks completion, often with unwanted consequences.
In Text: トランスレーションしてしまうと (if you end up translating); やる気もなくなっちゃいます (motivation disappears — contracted ちゃう form); 地面が氷になってしまって (the ground turns to ice); 運動不足な状態が続いてしまって (being in a lack-of-exercise state continues); ほぼ無くなってしまった (almost completely disappeared).
Note: N4. The host uses てしまう in its negative-consequence sense: "if you translate, you end up in a bad state." The contracted ちゃう form (やる気もなくなっちゃいます) is the spoken default. Self-learners learn てしまう from textbooks as "doing something completely" but the dominant use in this episode is the "unwanted result" frame. Shadow the トランスレーションしてしまうと chain; it's the warning that anchors the episode's main argument.

4. 〜という形で（〜という形）
Meaning: "In the form of ~ / in that shape." Noun + という + 形 + で — describes the format or structure of something.
In Text: という形で目に入っているもの (things that enter your eyes in that form); という形で調べていただいて (please look up in that form); という形でご紹介をしました (I introduced in that form); という形で終わりにします (I'll end in that form).
Note: N4. という形で is the host's go-to phrase for describing "the way something is structured." It's a meta-language marker — he uses it to talk about how he's presenting information. Self-learners rarely use this phrase but it's essential for describing formats, methods, or structures. Shadow the という形で + verb pattern; it's how Japanese speakers describe "the way I'm doing this."

5. 〜じゃないかな（柔らかい推測）
Meaning: "I wonder if ~ / probably ~." A soft-assertion hedge that states an opinion while leaving room for the listener's view.
In Text: ポイントじゃないかなと思います (I think the key point is probably); いきなり日本語で考えるのがポイントじゃないかなと思います (I think the key point is probably to think in Japanese right away); 結構珍しくなってきている (it's become quite rare); 結構いらっしゃるかなと思うんですけれども (I think there are probably quite a few).
Note: N4. じゃないかな is the most important soft-assertion pattern that self-learners under-use. Compare: きっと〜です (certainly is) → 〜じゃないですか (isn't it?) → 〜じゃないかな (I wonder?). The じゃないかな form is safest for cultural contexts because it makes no claims about facts, only about guesses. The host uses it 4+ times in this episode alone. Self-learners who don't internalize じゃないかな end up sounding overconfident. Shadow the ポイントじゃないかなと思います chunk; it's the most natural way to state an opinion in Japanese.
