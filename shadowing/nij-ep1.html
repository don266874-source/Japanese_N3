Natural Japanese (Beginner) - Ep1: Calling an Izakaya to Make a Reservation

Felix's Learner Note:

There's a moment every Japanese learner dreads, and it almost never happens in a textbook. You pick up the phone. A stranger answers in fast, native Japanese. Your heart pounds, your mind blanks, and the phrases you drilled for months suddenly vanish. This episode puts you right inside that moment — a learner calling an izakaya to book a table for six — and lets you hear, beat by beat, exactly how a real reservation call unfolds: the nervous self-talk before dialing, the wind drowning out your voice, the clerk's words you don't quite catch ("日時ってなんですか?"), the patient back-and-forth until the booking finally lands.

What makes this material gold for shadowing is that it's functional language, not topic language. You're not learning how to talk about the weather or your hobbies — you're learning how to get something done in Japanese. 電話で予約をしたいです. 風の音が大きいです. もう一度いいですか? 日時ってなんですか? 何名様ってどういう意味ですか? Every line is a transferable survival phrase you'll reuse the next time you call a restaurant, a clinic, a haircut appointment. And the vowel-rhythm of phone Japanese — softer, more hedged, full of すみません and ちょっと待っててくださいね — is completely different from the crisp textbook dialogue most learners have wired into their mouth. Shadowing this episode trains your tongue for the register of real phone politeness, which is the gap that sinks even N3-pass holders when they first try to book anything by phone in Japan.

Pay special attention to the conditional pattern at the end: １５分遅れるとキャンセルになります — "if you're 15 minutes late, the reservation is cancelled." That single ～と + result structure is the hinge of the whole episode, and it's the kind of cause-and-effect Japanese you'll use for the rest of your life. Shadow the full arc — nervous dial, confused clarifications, polite confirmation — and the next time your phone rings with a Japanese number on it, your body will already know what to do.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. ～と（自然条件・必然結果）
Meaning: "If ~, then ~ (naturally)." The conditional と marks a condition that automatically produces a result — no volition, no choice. Unlike ～たら (one-time trigger) or ～ば (general condition), と implies the result follows as surely as cause follows effect.
In Text: １５分遅れるとキャンセルになります / １５分遅れると、予約はなくなりますか？ (If you're 15 minutes late, the reservation is cancelled / If we're 15 minutes late, the reservation is gone?).
Note: N4. Japanese learners overuse ～たら for everything because textbooks teach it first. But the clerk here uses と for a reason: cancellation isn't a hypothetical — it's the automatic rule. １５分遅れたらキャンセルになります sounds like "if you happened to be late, the reservation might be cancelled"; １５分遅れるとキャンセルになります sounds like "be late 15 minutes and the system cancels you, no exceptions." Self-learners trying to state a policy, a schedule rule, or a law should reach for と first. Shadow this exact sentence — it's the cleanest demonstration of と's "automatic consequence" feel in the wild.

2. ～放題（～ほうだい）
Meaning: "All-you-can-~ / as much as you want." Suffixes onto the verb stem (飲み + 放題, 食べ + 放題, 読み + 放題) to express unlimited action within a set condition — usually time or price.
In Text: 飲み放題はプラス１５００円ですね / 飲み放題もその日に注文できますか？ / １時間半、たくさん飲めます (All-you-can-drink is +1,500 yen / Can we also order all-you-can-drink on the day? / You can drink a lot for an hour and a half).
Note: N3. This is one of the highest-frequency words in Japanese dining culture — 飲み放題 appears on almost every izakaya menu, and 食べ放題 on every buffet sign. Self-learners often misread it as "free drinks" because 放題 looks like 放 (release/free). It's not free — it's "all-you-can" for the stated price. The episode spells this out perfectly: １時間半で１５００円 (1,500 yen for 90 minutes of unlimited drinks). Note the verb-stem + 放題 pattern: 飲み放題 (drink), 食べ放題 (eat), 読み放題 (read). It's a productive suffix — once you learn it, you can coin 飲み放題, 食べ放題, 歌い放題 (all-you-can-sing), 見放題 (all-you-can-watch, streaming). This is exactly the kind of high-value cultural-linguistic chunk you'll use every time you enter aJapanese restaurant.

3. ～っていう意味です / ～っていうのは（口語定義・話題提示）
Meaning: "It means ~" / "By 'X' I mean ~." Spoken contraction of という (という → っていう). Used to define or rephrase a word that the listener may not understand.
In Text: 日時ってなんですか？ / 「いつ来ますか？」「何時に来ますか？」っていう意味です。 / 何名様ってどういう意味ですか？ (What does 'nichiji' mean? / It means 'when are you coming? what time are you coming?' / What does 'nan-mei-sama' mean?).
Note: N3 (spoken)/N4 (formal という). This episode is a masterclass in asking for clarification — the core learner survival skill. The listener doesn't nod and fake it; they ask, directly: X ってなんですか / X ってどういう意味ですか. Note the two spoken contractions: (1) という → っていう, textbook to real speech; (2) という意味ですか → っていう意味ですか, even more casual. Self-learners often freeze when they don't understand a word on a phone call; shadowing these three phrases — X ってなんですか, X ってどういう意味ですか, もう一度いいですか — gives you three polite retries for any phone situation. The register here is key: these work with strangers on the phone because they use です, not だ.

4. ～てみる / ～てみよう（試行・トライ）
Meaning: "Try doing ~ / give ~ a try." て-form of verb + みる (literally "see") — indicates attempting an action to see what happens. Volitional form てみよう is the "let's try" collaborative version.
In Text: 居酒屋CIJの電話番号を調べてみよう / 電話してみよう (Let me try looking up the number for Izakaya CIJ / Let's try giving them a call).
Note: N4. てみる is one of the most under-taught patterns in beginner Japanese, yet it appears constantly in natural speech. Compare 電話する (I'll call) vs 電話してみる (I'll try calling — with no guarantee it works out). The nuance is humility and tentativeness, exactly the tone of someone who's nervous about the outcome. Self-learners often say 電話します — confident, decisive — which sounds stiff when talking to yourself before a scary call. 電話してみよう sounds like "let's give it a shot" — the natural self-talk of a learner gearing up. The whole arc of this episode — 調べてみよう → 電話してみよう — is shadowable as one emotional rhythm: gathering courage through small trials. Internalize this and you'll sound like a Japanese speaker managing their own hesitancy, not a textbook reciting verb forms.

5. ～てくださいね / ～てね（柔らかい依頼・共感）
Meaning: "Please do ~, won't you?" The て-form + くださいね / てね softens a request with warmth — the ね at the end isn't filler, it's a particle that invites shared feeling, turning a command into something closer to "you and I both know this is reasonable, right?"
In Text: ちょっと待っててくださいね / 時間通りに来てください / ちょっと待っててくださいね (Just a moment, please / Please arrive on time / Just a moment, please).
Note: N5. The clerk's tone throughout the call is a clinic in phone-politeness, and the てくださいね form is the spine of it. Compare: ちょっと待ってください (wait a moment — cold command) vs ちょっと待っててくださいね (just a moment, please — warm, indulgent). The second one is what real Japanese service workers use. The trailing ね marks "I'm being considerate, and I trust you'll extend the same." Self-learners who only know textbook てください sound curt on the phone; those who pick up てくださいね sound patient and considerate — exactly the register a stranger on the other end of the line expects. Shadow this whole sentence and you'll have the anchors of a Japanese phone register down: clarify (もう一度いいですか), wait (ちょっと待っててくださいね), confirm (はい、そうですね), and request (時間通りに来てください).
