Natural Japanese (Beginner) - Ep2: Unboxing New Earphones

Felix's Learner Note:

If you've ever unboxed anything in Japan a phone, a kitchen gadget, a pair of earphones — you already know the experience is half product, half language. The box arrives, the 説明書 falls out, the 保証書 needs filing, the Bluetooth needs pairing, and somewhere in the middle of it all you realize the Japanese you learned from textbooks has almost nothing to do with the Japanese you need to use a thing. This episode captures that exact moment. A learner named Seika unboxes two new pairs of earphones on camera, and every line she speaks is the kind of functional, narration-over-action Japanese that textbooks almost never model.

What makes this material unusually good for shadowing is that it's process language — Japanese narrated in real time as the speaker does something physical. 蓋を開けますね. 保証書が入っています. 説明書は使い方が書いてあるものですね. プラスのボタンを押すと音量が大きくなります. This is the register you need for cooking videos, DIY tutorials, makeup walkthroughs, every "let me show you how this works" situation in Japanese life. And the cultural detail is gold: the liberation of finally having both right and left earphones after losing one AirPod, the reasoning behind buying a tethered pair vs a wireless pair for different use-cases, the throwaway うん、まいっか when the color turns out wrong — all of it is the unseen texture of Japanese daily narration.

Pay attention to two things while shadowing. First, the conditional-when pattern: 蓋を開けると見てください — "when you open the lid, look." That's the ～と of natural sequence, the exact structure you need for any "if/when X happens, Y follows" narration. Second, the ～てしまう pattern her lost AirPods: 右耳用のイヤホンは無くしてしまいました / どこかで落としてしまいました. ～てしまう carries the regret and finality that plain past tense doesn't — "I lost it, and it's gone." Shadow this whole episode once and you'll have the Japanese for unboxing, narrating, and ruefully admitting mistakes — three skills every learner eventually needs out loud.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. ～てしまう / ～てしまいました（完了・残念・不可逆）
Meaning: "Ended up doing ~ / did ~ (with a sense of finality or regret)." The て-form of a verb + しまう (to put away, to finish) marks an action as completed — and in most spoken contexts carries a tone of regret, accident, or lost control.
In Text: 右耳用のイヤホンは無くしてしまいました / どこかで落としてしまいました / 何度もこのエアポッズの片方を無くしてしまったので (I lost the right-side earphone / I dropped it somewhere / because I've lost one side of the AirPods so many times).
Note: N4. This is the single most useful "oops" structure in Japanese, and self-learners underuse it badly. Compare: 右のイヤホンは無くしました (I lost the right earphone — flat statement of fact) vs 右のイヤホンは無くしてしまいました (I lost the right earphone — and there's a little sting in it). The しまう verb literally means "to put away / finish," and grammatically it marks completion, but spoken Japanese uses it overwhelmingly for things that happened against the speaker's will. Seika uses it three times in one breath — 無くしてしまいました / 落としてしまいました / 無くしてしまった — because losing the AirPod feels like exactly that: an irreversible accident. Self-learners who want to say "I accidentally ~" should reach for てしまう before any fancy adverb. Shadow these three sentences together and you'll have the natural Japanese for "I messed up and now it's gone."

2. ～と（自然順序・「～すると～」の結果）
Meaning: "When ~ happens, ~ happens" — the conditional と marks a natural sequence: an action triggers a result automatically, as cause follows effect.
In Text: 蓋を開けると、見てください / プラスのボタンを押すと音量が大きくなります / マイナスのボタンを押すと音量が小さくなります (When you open the lid, look / When you press the plus button, the volume goes up / When you press the minus button, the volume goes down).
Note: N4. This is the conditional for narrating process — exactly the register of unboxing videos, cooking walkthroughs, assembly instructions. Notice the two flavors in this episode: (1) 蓋を開けると見てください — "when you open the lid (and you will), look" — the と here is a temporal/sequential "when"; (2) ボタンを押すと音量が大きくなります — "if/when you press the button, the volume goes up" — the と here is the automatic input→output rule. Self-learners often default to ～たら for everything ("if ~ happens"), but Japanese speakers reach for と when the connection is reliable and predictable — a button press, a lid opening, a switch flipping. Shadow the button-press sentences as a pair; they're a complete template for any "control X → result Y" explanation in Japanese.

3. ～ことにします / ～ことにしました
Meaning: "I decided to ~." Takes the dictionary form of a verb ( affirmative) or ない form (negative) + ことにする / ことにしました — expresses a deliberate decision the speaker made.
In Text: 繋がっているイヤホンを買うことにしました / bluetoothのものにしました / 私はMサイズにしようと思います / 大切に使っていこうと思います (I decided to buy a tethered pair / I went with a Bluetooth one / I think I'll go with M / I intend to take good care of them).
Note: N4. Decision language is critical for self-learners — it's how you explain why you did something, in Japanese, to Japanese speakers. Seika cycles through three variations in one episode: 買うことにしました (past decision — "I decided to buy"), しようと思います (present leaning — "I'm thinking of doing"), していこうと思います (ongoing commitment — "I intend to keep doing"). The nuance ladder: ことにしました = decision already locked in; しようと思います = leaning toward, still soft; していこうと思います = continuous commitment going forward. Self-learners who only know します ("I will do") sound blunt and overconfident; those who pick up ことにしました / しようと思います sound like a person weighing options and choosing — exactly the register of a thoughtful consumer, which is most of human life.

4. ～てある / ～てあります
Meaning: "Is written / is done (and remains in that state)." て-form of verb + ある marks that an action was performed by someone, and the result is still visible / still true right now. Subject is inanimate.
In Text: ケースの前にはハートの絵文字が書いてあります / 説明書はイヤホンの使い方が書いてあるものですね / ここにRとL書いてあります / 日本語で書いてあります (On the front of the case a heart emoji is drawn / The manual is a thing where the earphone usage is written / Here it says R and L / It's written in Japanese).
Note: N4. This is a deceptively tricky point because textbooks teach 書いてあります ("is written") in isolation, but in real speech it's used constantly — every time you read a sign, a label, an instruction, a tattoo. Compare: ハートが書きました (the heart wrote — wrong, hearts don't write) vs ハートが書いてあります (a heart is drawn [and is right there]) — the second is correct because the subject is the result, not the agent. Self-learners often wrongly sayEnglish-style "It writes R" (Rを書きます) when they mean "It says R" (Rと書いてあります). Seika uses 書いてあります four times across this short video — that's how common it is. Shadow the pattern 「X が / と 書いてあります」 as a fixed chunk: this is the Japanese for "it says X" / "X is written there."

5. ～てみる / ～てみよう
Meaning: "Try doing ~ / give ~ a try." Verb て-form + みる (literally "see") — marks an attempt, an experimental action taken to see what happens.
In Text: 開けようと思います / ちょっとつけてみますね / ちょっと押してみます / 開けてみます / スマホと繋げようと思います / 買ってみました / 使ってみようと思います (I'm thinking of opening it / let me try putting them on / let me try pressing it / let me try opening it / I'm thinking of pairing it with my phone / I tried buying them / I think I'll try using them).
Note: N4. This is the most under-taught high-frequency pattern in beginner Japanese. Watch how often Seika reaches for てみる instead of flat する: つけます ("I'll put them on") sounds decisive and final; つけてみます ("let me try putting them on") leaves room for the outcome to surprise her. The nuance is humility and curiosity — the exact tone you want when you don't know what'll happen next. Note also the volitional てみよう form: 開けよう / 繋げよう / 使っていこう — "let's try ~ing." This is the collaborative "let's give it a shot" tone, perfect for vlogs and self-talk. Self-learners who never pick up てみる sound like they're announcing completed intentions; those who master it sound like learners experimenting with the world, which is exactly what a Japanese learner is.
