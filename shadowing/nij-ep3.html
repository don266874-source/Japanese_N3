Natural Japanese (Beginner) - Ep3: Curry🍛

Felix's Learner Note:

What makes this material unusually good for shadowing is the repetition loop. Seika narrates the same sequence — 作りました, 食べました, 美味しかったです, 大変でした — three times over: once forward as it happens, once as photos, once as a recap. Self-learners need exactly this. Spoken Japanese is not a list of distinct sentences; it's the same handful of patterns looping with small substitutions, and the only way to acquire that rhythm is to shadow a native speaker who is herself looping. Seika does this naturally, and the result is that anyone who shadows twenty minutes of her voice will end up owning the Japanese for "I made it, I ate it, it was good, it was tough" — the skeleton of every food and life story you'll ever tell.

Pay attention to three things while shadowing. First, the necessity structure: 一人で食べなければいけません — "I have to eat it alone." The ～なければいけません form is self-learners' single most underused tool for describing obligation softly and naturally. Second, the discovery ～たら: 「いただきます」と言ったら猫が来ました — "the moment I said 'thanks for the food,' the cat showed up." The ～たら of "X happened, and then to my surprise Y" is the structure for every anecdote you'll ever tell in Japanese. Third, the noun-phrase emphasis: 一人で全部食べるのは大変でした — "eating it all by myself was tough." The ～のは construction lets you make a verb phrase the topic — and this is how Japanese speakers actually comment on their own actions. Shadow the whole episode once and you'll have the Japanese for cooking, eating, suffering through leftovers, and laughing at yourself about it — four skills every learner eventually needs out loud.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. ～なければいけません（義務・必然）
Meaning: "Have to ~ / must ~." Negative conditional な ければ (if not) + いけません (won't do / is no good) — describes an obligation, something the speaker must do.
In Text: このカレー全部、私が一人で食べなければいけません (I have to eat all of this curry, all by myself).
Note: N4. This is the single most underused structure among self-learners who need to describe obligation in spoken Japanese. Compare the formal しなければなりません of textbooks ("must do") with the conversational しなければいけません of real speech — Seika uses いけません, the everyday register, not なりません. The nuance here is critical: she's not saying she wants to eat the curry alone, she's saying she has to eat it alone — because her husband won't and her cat can't. The なければ form is the engine of Japanese obligation language; once you own it, you can talk about everything from "I have to study Japanese" to "I have to call my mom" without sounding like a textbook. Shadow this one sentence; the pattern 〇〇を一人で食べなければいけません is a fixed chunk you can reuse for any "I have to ~ alone" scenario.

2. ～ていました（描写・状態の持続）
Meaning: "Was ~ing / kept ~ing." Verb て-form + いました (past progressive / state-duration) — describes an ongoing past state, often used for narration of background action.
In Text: ぐつぐつぐつぐつ煮込んでいます / テーブルの上でもまだぐつぐつ言っています / ぐつぐつぐつぐつ言っていましたね (it's simmering and simmering and simmering / even on the table it's still bubbling and making noise / it was going bub-bub-bub-bub, wasn't it).
Note: N4. The ています / ていました pair is how Japanese narrates ongoing background action — exactly the register of a cooking video. Onomatopoeia gets glued to the verb with て: ぐつぐつ煮込んでいます literally "it's simmer-simmer-simmering." Self-learners often incorrectly use the past tense 煮込みました ("it simmered") when the visual scene is clearly ongoing; the mistake flattens the description. Notice also how Seika switches mid-narrative from present observation (ぐつぐつ言っています) to retrospective narration in the recap (ぐつぐつ言っていましたね) — same verb, same scene, different temporal stance, exactly as native speakers do when recounting something they just showed you. Shadow the onomatopoeia + ています chunk as a unit; it's how Japanese speakers describe anything alive and in motion.

3. ～たら（発見・「～した時、～が起きた」）
Meaning: "When ~ happened, ~ happened (to my surprise / as it turned out)." The past-tense たら form marks either a temporal trigger or an unexpected discovery — "the moment X happened, I found that Y."
In Text: 「いただきます」と言ったら猫が来ました (the moment I said "let's eat," the cat showed up); パソコンでカレーの写真を見ていたら「カレーが食べたい！」カレーが食べたくなりました (I was looking at curry photos on my computer and — "I want to eat curry!" — I wanted to eat curry).
Note: N4. This is the structure every Japanese anecdote is built on. Textbook usually drills たら as simple past conditional ("if you do X, then Y"), but in speech the most common use is the discovery たら: you did X, and then unexpectedly Y came into the picture. Seika uses it twice in this short episode — once for the cat suddenly appearing, once for her own craving suddenly arising. The key tell: the second clause is almost always something the speaker didn't plan or expect. Compare 保証書を見たら壊れた (if I look at the warranty, it breaks — meaningless) vs パソコンでカレーの写真を見ていたらカレーが食べたくなりました (I was looking at curry photos, and then — oh — I wanted curry — natural). Self-learners who can use たら this way unlock the ability to tell stories in Japanese instead of reporting facts.

4. ～のは（名詞化・話題の強調）
Meaning: "Doing ~ is (something)." Verb (short form) + の + は turns an action phrase into a noun phrase, then marks it as the topic — lets you make a comment about doing X.
In Text: 一人で全部食べるのは大変でした (eating it all by myself was tough); 一人で全部食べるのはちょっと、ちょっと大変でした (eating it all by myself was a little, a little tough).
Note: N4. The の nominalizer is the structure textbook courses drill least and native speakers use most. Compare: 一人で食べました、大変でした ("I ate alone, it was tough" — comma splice, two flat statements) vs 一人で食べるのは大変でした ("Eating alone — that was tough" — the action itself is the topic, the tough-ness is the comment). Seika uses this construction twice in close succession — the repeat itself does thework of emphasis. Self-learners tend to render their own actions as "I did X, X was adjective" instead of "Doing X was adjective" — the second is how Japanese speakers actually center their own actions for comment. Shadow 「〇〇するのは形容詞でした」as a fixed template; it's how you describe your own experience as a topic instead of as a sequence of flat events.

5. ～ていません / 好きじゃありません（否定状態・「～ない」の丁寧形）
Meaning: "Doesn't ~ / is not ~" — the polite negative of state verbs and な-adjectives. Negative of ます (ます → ません) for verb states; negative of です (です → じゃありません / ではありません) for な-adjective and noun predicates.
In Text: 夫はカレーは食べません / 好きじゃありません / でも猫は食べられません / できません (My husband doesn't eat curry / he doesn't like it / but the cat can't eat it / it's not an option for the cat).
Note: N5→N4. The negative is the single most underperformed area for English-speaking self-learners, because English "do + not" and Japanese verb-negative conjugation are structurally different. Seika's husband sequence is the whole grammar in miniature: 夫は食べません (verb-negative — "doesn't eat") → 好きじゃありません (な-adjective negative — "is not liked"). Same content complement ("curry"), two different negative constructions, mirroring the difference between action negation and property negation. Also note the affection-pressure sequence for the cat: 猫は食べられません (potential negative — "can't eat" — physical impossibility) vs できません (general potential — "can't / not an option"). Shadow all four sentences together; they're a complete set: not doing, not liking, not being able to, not being possible — the entire Japanese negative kit in one episode.
