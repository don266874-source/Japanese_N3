Natural Japanese (Beginner) - Ep4: Conversation at the Hospital

Felix's Learner Note:

What makes this material unusually good for shadowing is that it's functional spoken Japanese in a high-stakes register. Nobody goes to a clinic for fun. When you're there, you need to be precise, you need to be polite, and you need to understand what's said back to you. Seika walks through the entire visit — symptom self-check at home, greeting the reception, answering どうされましたか, describing pain type by type, taking the prescription, picking up the medicine at the pharmacy, and confirming dosing for morning-noon-evening-after-meal vs before-meal. It's the complete loop. And the language is the exact register Japanese clinicians use: そうなんですね, それは辛いですね, お間違いないですか — every phrase a small empathy deposit that you, the patient, are supposed to receive and recognize.

Pay attention to three things while shadowing. First, the conjecture ~かもしれません: 熱があるのかもしれない / 薬がもらえるかもしれない / エアコンをつけて寝たので喉が乾燥したのかもしれません — the "maybe" structure that lets you hypothesize causes and outcomes gently, the spine of every diagnostic conversation. Second, the done-it-to-myself ~てしまう: 風邪を引いてしまいました / 夏風邪を引いてしまったんですね — regret baked into the verb, the natural register for "I caught a cold (and I'd rather not have)." Third, the manner-question どのように痛いですか — "how does it hurt?" — and its onomatopoeic answers ズキズキ / ヒリヒリ. Self-learners who can describe pain in Japanese are rare; shadow this whole episode once and you won't be one of them.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. ～かもしれません（推量・可能性）
Meaning: "Maybe ~ / might ~." Plain form of verb/い-adjective, or な-adjective stem / noun + かもしれません — marks a conjecture or possibility, softer than でしょう and more honestly uncertain.
In Text: 熱があるのかもしれないなあ (I might have a fever) / 薬がもらえるかもしれない (I might be able to get medicine) / エアコンをつけて寝たので喉が乾燥したのかもしれません (I slept with the AC on, so my throat might have dried out) / そうかもしれませんね (that might be the case).
Note: N4. This is the single most useful "maybe" structure in spoken Japanese, and self-learners underuse it badly because 〜でしょう ("probably") feels more polished. The difference is honesty: でしょうか / かもしれません is "I genuinely don't know, but maybe," while でしょう is "I'm fairly confident." A doctor saying そうかもしれませんね is being deliberately non-committal — the diagnosis isn't locked in. Notice Seika uses it four times in 30 seconds across the hypothetical-fever / maybe-medicine / maybe-AC / doctor-responding sequence. The ～かもしれません form is the engine of every medical conversation: doctors propose causes, patients propose explanations, both sides hedge. Self-learners who can't hedge sound overconfident; those who pick up かもしれません sound like a person reasoning out loud — exactly the register clinical dialogue runs on. Shadow 「〇〇のかもしれません / 〇〇かもしれませんね」 as a fixed chunk; it's how Japanese speakers say "maybe" without losing face.

2. ～てしまう / ～てしまいました（完了・不可逆・残念）
Meaning: "Ended up doing ~ / did ~ (with a sting of regret or accident)." Verb て-form + しまう (literally "to put away / finish") — marks completion, and in most spoken contexts carries a tone of "I did it, and now it's done, and I'd rather not."
In Text: 風邪を引いてしまいました (I caught a cold) / 今日は夏風邪を引いてしまったんですね (so you've come down with a summer cold, huh). 風邪引いちゃったんだ (I caught a cold — the contracted ちゃった form, spoken to oneself).
Note: N4. The same てしまう pattern as in the unboxing episode, but here the register is medical — a doctor-patient exchange where てしまう does the emotional work of saying "I got sick and I didn't mean to." Compare: 風邪を引きました (I caught a cold — flat report) vs 風邪を引いてしまいました (I caught a cold — and it's a little unfortunate that I did). Doctors tend to mirror the patient's てしまう back at them: 今日は夏風邪を引いてしまったんですね — the doctor isn't saying the patient did anything wrong, but is acknowledging the regret,embedding sympathetic tone into the verb itself. Also notice the spoken contracted form 風邪引いちゃったんだ that Seika uses while talking to herself at home before going to the clinic — the exact register you use when nobody else is listening. Three forms, one function: polite (引いてしまいました), plain (引いてしまった), contracted (引いちゃった). Self-learners who learn only てしまいました from textbooks miss the ちゃった form every Japanese person actually uses in self-talk.

3. ～か／～か（選択疑問・二者択一）
Meaning: "Is it A, or is it B?" — two questions joined by か, presenting the listener with a binary choice.
In Text: ズキズキしますか？ それともヒリヒリしますか？ (Does it throb? Or does it sting?); 普通に呼吸をしていて大丈夫ですか？ / はい、大丈夫ですよ (Are you OK breathing normally? / Yes, fine).
Note: N5→N4. This is diagnostic-question bread and butter. Notice the それとも ("or rather") that links the two pain-type options — それとも picks between exactly two alternatives, doesn't extend to a third. Self-learners often confuse か / か ("or") with そりとも ("or rather") and あるいは ("alternatively"); the doctor's それとも ヒリヒリしますか is the right word because the choice is binary and the doctor wants the patient to commit to one type. Also note ズキズキ / ヒリヒリ — two onomatopoeia for two different pain types: ズキズキ is the throb of a headache or toothache, ヒリヒリ is the sting of a burn or a dried-out throat. English doesn't have a clean translation; the onomatopoeia is the diagnosis. Shadow the ズキズキしますか / それとも ヒリヒリしますか pair as a unit; it's how Japanese clinicians narrow a symptom by asking the patient to choose between vividly imagined sensations.

4. ～てみよう（試行の意志・「～してみよう」）
Meaning: "Let me try doing ~ / let's try doing ~." Volitional form of みる (to try) attached to verb て-form — marks an attempt the speaker is committing to, often as self-talk or a soft proposal.
In Text: 体温計で熱を測ってみよう (Let me try measuring my temperature with the thermometer) / 熱を測ってみよう (Let me try taking my temperature) / 病院に行ってみよう (Let me try going to the hospital).
Note: N4. The volitional てみよう is the spoken-Japanese register for self-directed trial. Compare: 熱を測ります ("I'll measure my temperature" — decisive, final tone) vs 熱を測ってみよう ("Let me try measuring my temperature" — exploring, the outcome not yet known). Seika uses it three times in the first two minutes — 体温計で熱を測ってみよう, 熱を測ってみよう (repeated for rhythm), 病院に行ってみよう — each one the same grammatical move: I'm going to try X and see what happens. This is exactly the register you want when you're uncertain whether your action will work — going to the doctor when you don't know if they can help, taking medicine when you don't know if it'll fix you. Self-learners who only know てみます ("I will try") sound polite but stiff; those who pick up てみよう sound like a person quietly making up their mind. Shadow all three sentences; they're a template for any "I'll try X and see" action in Japanese daily life.

5. ～てくださいね / ～てください（依頼の丁寧さの差）
Meaning: "Please do ~." Verb て-form + ください — the soft imperative. Adding ね ("please, OK?") makes it warmer and more caring; dropping ね makes it a clean professional request.
In Text: 今日は家で安静にしていてください (Please rest quietly at home today) / 水をたくさん飲んでくださいね (Please drink a lot of water, OK?) / 処方箋を薬局に持って行ってくださいね (Please take the prescription to the pharmacy) / 食べられるものは食べてくださいね (Please eat whatever you can eat, OK?) / なるべく水で飲んでください (Please take it with water as much as possible) / 皆さんも風邪には気をつけてくださいね (Everyone, please be careful about catching a cold, too).
Note: N5. This is the easiest grammar on paper and the hardest to use naturally in real Japanese. The difference between 家で安静にしていてください and 水をたくさん飲んでくださいね is small in form but huge in tone — the first is a doctor's instruction (firm, professional), the second is caring advice (warm, repeated as if to a child). Seika uses てくださいね six times in the second half of the episode, almost every time with ね — because the doctor is giving caring advice, not just instructions. Self-learners who only learn てください sound clipped and officious in Japanese ("Drink water. Do this. Take this.") ; those who pick up てくださいね sound like a person who cares whether you do it. The ね is the particle of warmth. Shadow the six てください / てくださいね lines as a set; you'll hear the difference between "instruction Japanese" and "caring-Japanese Japanese" without anyone explaining it to you.

5. ～ていません / 好きじゃありません（否定状態・「～ない」の丁寧形）
Meaning: "Doesn't ~ / is not ~" — the polite negative of state verbs and な-adjectives. Negative of ます (ます → ません) for verb states; negative of です (です → じゃありません / ではありません) for な-adjective and noun predicates.
In Text: 夫はカレーは食べません / 好きじゃありません / でも猫は食べられません / できません (My husband doesn't eat curry / he doesn't like it / but the cat can't eat it / it's not an option for the cat).
Note: N5→N4. The negative is the single most underperformed area for English-speaking self-learners, because English "do + not" and Japanese verb-negative conjugation are structurally different. Seika's husband sequence is the whole grammar in miniature: 夫は食べません (verb-negative — "doesn't eat") → 好きじゃありません (な-adjective negative — "is not liked"). Same content complement ("curry"), two different negative constructions, mirroring the difference between action negation and property negation. Also note the affection-pressure sequence for the cat: 猫は食べられません (potential negative — "can't eat" — physical impossibility) vs できません (general potential — "can't / not an option"). Shadow all four sentences together; they're a complete set: not doing, not liking, not being able to, not being possible — the entire Japanese negative kit in one episode.
