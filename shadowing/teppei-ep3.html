Nihongo con Teppei (Beginner) - Ep3: どこに住んでいますか？

Felix's Learner Note:

When I was living in Tokyo, the question that caught me off guard most often wasn't "what's your job?" or "what did you do this weekend?" — it was "where do you live?" Not because the words were hard, but because the answer came at me in a rapid-fire list of cities, countries, and particles that my textbook had never strung together at native speed. I'd learned に and で as separate grammar points. Nobody warned me that real speakers would rattle off "ロサンゼルスに住んでいます / 日本に住んでいますか" in a single breath and expect me to keep up.

For a self-learner with no Japanese neighbors, this is the closest thing to drilling with a native speaker who actually waits for your answer. Shadowing this loop until the に住んでいます pattern becomes automatic will do more for your spoken reflexes than memorizing particle charts. The episode also sneaks in high-value N3 grammar — もしかすると (perhaps), 〜かな (I wonder), 〜とか (among other things) — all embedded in natural, unscripted speech. Run it until Teppei's city list stops sounding like a geography quiz and starts sounding like a conversation you can actually join.

📚 Grammar Breakdown

1. ～に住んでいます (～にすんでいます)
Meaning: To live in/at (a place). The particle に marks the location where the state of living continues.
In Text: 僕は東京に住んでいます / ロンドンに住んでいますか / 日本に住んでいますか / 沖縄に住んでいます (I live in Tokyo / Do you live in London? / Do you live in Japan? / I live in Okinawa).
Note: N5 core pattern, but Teppei's episode reveals why learners still stumble: native speakers chain it with city names at speed, and the particle に gets swallowed in connected speech. Self-learners often confuse に (state/location of existence) with で (location of action) — 住む takes に because living is a *state*, not an action. Shadow this episode until に住んでいます becomes a single rhythmic unit, not three separate words.
Why "どこに住んでいますか" is a Major Trap for Beginners
"どこに住んでいますか" (Where do you live?) is actually one of the most common grammar traps for Japanese learners! Beginners almost always make two specific mistakes because of how they think in English.Here are the two classic mistakes and why they happen:

Mistake 1: Using the particle "で" instead of "に" ❌
  • What beginners say: どこに住みますか。
  • Why they make this mistake: Early on, students learn that "で" is the particle for action locations (e.g., "どこで食べますか / Where do you eat?"). Naturally, they think "living" is an action, so they reflexively use "で".
  • The correct logic: I Japanese, "住む (to live)" is not treated as a temporary action, but as a state of continuous existence. For places where someone or something permanently exists or stays, Japanese grammar strictly requires the particle "に".
Mistake 2: Saying "住みますか" instead of "住んでいますか" ❌
  • What beginners say: どこに住みますか。
  • Why they make this mistake: Beginners are used to using the simple "~ます" form for current habits or general truths (like "日本語を勉強します / I study Japanese").
  • The correct logic: If you say どこに住みますか, it actually means "Where are you going to move/live?" (talking about future plans). To ask where someone is currently settled down, you must use the "〜ています" form because it shows an ongoing, continuous state.

2. ～かな / ～ですかね
Meaning: "I wonder…?" / "…, I suppose?" — a softening particle that turns a direct question into a musing, making it sound less interrogative and more conversational.
In Text: 香港に住んでますかね / 日本のどこに住んでいますかね / 沖縄,沖縄いいですね (Do you live in Hong Kong, I wonder? / Where in Japan do you live, I suppose? / Okinawa — that's nice, isn't it).
Note: N4. かな is self-directed wondering ("I wonder if…"); ですかね projects that wondering outward with politeness. Teppei uses both constantly — かな when thinking out loud, ですかね when gently prompting the listener. Common mistake: learners use かな with superiors (too casual) or avoid it entirely and sound robotic. For self-learners, mastering かな/ですかね is the difference between "textbook robot" and "actual human."

3. ～もしかすると / ～もしかしたら
Meaning: Perhaps / maybe / possibly — introduces a speculation or guess the speaker isn't certain about.
In Text: ロシアですかもしかしたら / もしかすると日本に住んでいますか (Russia, perhaps? / Perhaps you live in Japan?).
Note: N3. Both もしかすると and もしかしたら are interchangeable; もしかしたra is slightly more colloquial. The key nuance: the speaker is *guessing*, not stating a fact. Learners often overuse たぶん (probably) when もしかすると is more appropriate — たぶん = high confidence guess; もしかすると = low confidence / just occurred to me. Teppei uses it to keep the question open-ended rather than demanding a specific answer.

4. ～とか
Meaning: "Such as / among other things / or something like that" — a casual listing particle that gives examples without exhausting the list.
In Text: ね都市（とし）とか町同じ意味ですねとし町 / ロサンジェルスとねいうよくねっ (Cities (toshi) and towns — same meaning, right? / Los Angeles and so on, you know).
Note: N4. とか is the single most common listing particle in spoken Japanese and the one textbooks under-teach. It implies "here are some examples, but there are more" — perfect for when you can't name everything. Learners tend to overuse や (and) in speech when とか sounds more natural and conversational. Teppei's とか usage is textbook native rhythm: drop it in, keep moving, don't over-explain.

5. ～てみたい
Meaning: "I want to try doing ~" — combines ～てみる (to try doing) with ～たい (want to) to express a desire to attempt something.
In Text: 今日はそれについて聞いてみたいと思います (Today I'd like to try asking about that / I want to give asking about that a try).
Note: N4. ～てみたい is softer and more exploratory than just ～たい — it carries the nuance of "I want to see what happens if I do this." Teppei uses 聞いてみたいと思います to frame the whole episode as an experiment: "Let's try asking this and see what comes back." Self-learners often skip the みたい layer and say 聞きたいです — grammatically fine, but missing the native speaker's instinct to soften intentions with "try."
